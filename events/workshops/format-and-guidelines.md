# CodeDay Workshops: Format and Guidelines

Since 2010, CodeDay organizers, mentors, and volunteers have run at least 2,000 workshops for 50,000+ students. Along the way, we've learned a lot about what works, and what doesn't, and we've put together this guide to share some pro-tips.

These are guidelines. Like everything at CodeDay, we encourage creativity in your workshop design, but try to follow them if you don't have a specific reason to deviate.

### Getting Your Workshop on the Schedule

Talk to your CodeDay contact about your specific slot on the schedule.

In general, workshops targeting beginners and intermediate students are best as close to the beginning of the event as possible.

### Technical Details for Online Workshops

- Online workshops will be hosted on Zoom (please make sure it's installed and updated).
- CodeDay will set up and host the webinar. We will need a title for your workshop, and a list of names and emails of everyone planning to present. Plan to arrive 10 minutes early for a tech check.
- You will be able to screen-share. If you need to share audio, you should test this during the tech check or in advance.

- At CodeDay Labs we will introduce you. At Virtual CodeDay you will be entirely in charge of kicking off your own workshop and Q&A. Please plan to start no more than 3 minutes late.

## What's The Goal Of a Workshop?

When we're designing or booking workshops, one thing is most on our mind: what's going to happen after students leave the workshop. We always have one of two goals:

### Beginner- and Intermediate-Focused Workshops

**Students should leave with an immediate, obvious next step.** Your workshop is the beginning to their CodeDay project's story.

For example, in our workshop for total beginners, every student learns to make a platforming game about [Bill Gates jumping over a chair,](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QqfjiuqVrV4) but we end the workshop by brainstorming ideas for new game mechanics. Make it rain chairs? A chair that shoots other chairs? Enemy chairs that track Bill Gates? Multiple levels?

![](../Workshops%20and%20More/CodeDay%20Workshops%20Format%20and%20Guidelines/Untitled%201.png)

When student leave a beginner workshop, we want their mind to be swimming with new ideas that they're excited to implement right away. Because these students are often not super committed to programming yet, this helps us keep their engagement high.

{: .note }
> **Designing Your Workshop For Success**
>
> We recognize that most workshops need to convey some technical content. Students can't build a creative game without knowing how to implement movement, for example. It's hard to make an entire workshop creative and inspiring in a scalable way.
>
> Here are four tips everyone can apply:
>
> 1. **Remove some content.** Instead of teaching students how to do *everything*, teach them how to do *the next thing*. Students have constant access to individual mentors who can help them with the next step. (More on this later.)
>
> 2. **Frame it around a project/demo.** CodeDay is about building something, and beginner students aren't great at understanding how abstract concepts will help them. Replacing your slides with a project or demo you help them create is a big help.
>
> 3. **Answer the unspoken, "why should I care?".** Set the right tone by starting off with inspiring examples of creative projects they could make with the knowledge you're going to give them. When students want to learn for their own interest, they're very good at it.
>
> 4. **End with ideas for what's next.** Brainstorming some next-steps gets students excited to go out and actually implement them!

### Advanced and Non-Technical Workshops

![](../Workshops%20and%20More/CodeDay%20Workshops%20Format%20and%20Guidelines/Untitled%202.png)

Students should leave knowing **how and when to apply the subject to their projects,** and **the first step to take when they need it.**

Unlike giving a tech talk to professional software engineers, students at CodeDay aren't equipped to understand the fine details of most subjects.

For example, instead of going into the details of how Kubernetes works, most students will get more out of a workshop explaining what Kubernetes does and why it's valuable, and how they can try it out in their own projects.

As with beginner- and intermediate-focused workshops, it's helpful if you can use real demos for our advanced students, although slides with technical diagrams, videos, and whiteboarding is appropriate for this audience in moderation.

## Teaching Less Gets Better Outcomes

Research we've conducted with CodeDay participants suggests that teaching less concepts — even those critical to creating a successful project — often leads to better results!

![](../Workshops%20and%20More/CodeDay%20Workshops%20Format%20and%20Guidelines/Untitled%203.png)

Because so many of our students are new to coding, providing too much information is not only overwhelming, it's *scary* — it reinforces their misconceptions about coding being a hard field that needs years of study!

How do you decide what to remove? We recommend *only covering technical knowledge that's critical to reducing immediate frustrations.*

## Time Management

A few tips to keep in mind as you plan out your workshop:

- Beginner workshops tend to have most questions come up during the content, with little saved for the end. Advanced workshops are the opposite. Plan your Q&A length accordingly.
- If you're teaching a hands-on workshop to total beginners *(especially at an in-person event)* plan to be interrupted frequently. If you need volunteers to help students solve problems, your CodeDay contact can help you arrange that.
