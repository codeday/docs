# Grants to Individuals

# What is a grant?

A grant is an amount of money given for a particular purpose which primarily benefits the grantee. For example:

<aside>
✅

**Grants**

- Scholarships *(amounts paid to students attending an educational institution for the purposes of study)*
- Fellowships *(short-term opportunities which focus on the professional development of the fellow)*
- Prizes
- Awards
</aside>

<aside>
🚫

**Not Grants**

- Wages, salaries, or other compensation for performing a service
- Payments for teaching or research which primarily benefits the organization and not the grantee
</aside>

# Requirements for Individual Grants

## Grants must be a defined program which serve a charitable class of individuals.

A grant cannot be created to assist particular, pre-selected individuals. Grants must be for a “charitable class”, meaning the group of people who are eligible for the grant must be large enough to benefit the larger community. Per the IRS:

> *A charitable class must be either large enough that the potential beneficiaries cannot be individually identified, or sufficiently indefinite that the community as a whole, rather than a pre-selected group of people, benefits when a charity provides assistance. For example, a charitable class could consist of all individuals located in a city, county, or state. This charitable class is large and benefits to it benefit the entire geographic community.*
> 

## Grants must be charitable and align with CodeDay’s mission.

Charity in this case is used as a well-defined legal term. Most grants which are both charitable and mission-aligned will fit into one of these categories.

- Scientific research in the public interest
- Educational
- Elimination of prejudice and discrimination
- Relief of the poor and distressed

## Grants must have documented procedures which seem likely to achieve charitable outcomes.

Before launching an individual grant program, we must document selection and reporting procedures which are likely to result in charitable outcomes which meet the following requirements:

1. The criteria used to select grantees must reasonably relate to the charitable reason for making the grant, and be objective and nondiscriminatory.
2. The people selecting recipients must not be in a position to derive a private benefit from their selection (even indirectly).
3. Grantees must report on whether they performed the activities the promised activities.
4. These selection and reporting procedures should be reasonably likely to result in grantees performing the activities that the grants are intended to finance.
5. Renewal grants must verify that required reports were submitted and that we have no reason to believe the funding was used other than for the purpose for which it was awarded.

# Grants likely have tax implications.

Grants for tuition or required books for students who are candidates for a degree at a tax-exempt institution of higher education. (Documentation of how the money is spent is required unless it’s paid directly to the educational institution.)

Most other forms of grants are taxable, and non-resident aliens (such as F-1/J-1 student visa holders) may be subject to withholding. Recipients who are not US citizens or permanent residents may not be legally able to accept grants.

For more information on eligibility, see: 

[Payments to Visa Holders](Payments%20to%20Visa%20Holders%20105cb48bff9680be946fc95428862476.md)

CodeDay needs to determine if we must comply with withholding for non-resident aliens, but otherwise is not responsible for determining the legal eligibility of grant recipients. CodeDay is required to report payments over $600 to US residents and nonresident aliens to the IRS.
