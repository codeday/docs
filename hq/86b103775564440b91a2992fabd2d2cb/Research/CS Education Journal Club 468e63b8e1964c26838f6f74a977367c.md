# CS Education Journal Club

If you aren’t already involved in conducting research, a great place to start is by joining our CS Education Journal Club!

# What is a Journal Club?

> A journal club is a regular gathering of scientists to discuss a scientific paper found in a research journal. One or two members of the club present a summary of the chosen paper that the whole group has read. Then, the discussion begins. Attendees ask clarifying questions, inquire about different aspects of the experimental design, critique the methods, and bring a healthy amount of skepticism (or praise) to the results.
> 

[https://irp.nih.gov/blog/post/2015/03/5-tips-for-journal-club-first-timers](https://irp.nih.gov/blog/post/2015/03/5-tips-for-journal-club-first-timers)

> There is debate over whether the main goal of a journal club should be for attendees to keep abreast of the literature, or to develop critical appraisal skills. […] A journal club may incorporate both or vary its aims over time.
> 

[https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8749379/](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8749379/)

# What will I do?

Every meeting, someone will be assigned to lead the discussion of a particular paper. If that’s you, see the section below, otherwise you need to:

- Read the paper prior to the meeting
- Try your best to understand it
- Be open about what you don’t understand
- Contribute questions and discussion topics during the meeting

# When it’s my turn to lead, what will I do?

First of all, read through the paper! You can also search online to see if it’s been discussed by anyone else.

You’ll need to prepare notes to facilitate a discussion using the format below. Please don’t make a powerpoint, though, this isn’t a lecture.

Here’s a recommended format for the discussion:

<aside>
📌 If there are things you don’t understand about the paper, ask the audience if they understand it. This doesn’t reflect poorly on you — many papers are unfortunately not written very clearly.

</aside>

- Summarize the paper in 1-2 sentences, and explain why this paper might be important or relevant.
- Describe the background and context. This might be a summary from a “background” or “related work” section near the beginning of the paper, but you should also include any context *you personally* have, even if the authors don’t mention it.
- What did the authors do? This is usually covered by a section/sections called “Program”, “Format”, “Methods”, “Empirical Setting”, or similar.
- Talk the audience through each figure and table, which are usually some of the most important parts.
- Summarize the author’s conclusions, and state whether you agree with them and why.

DON’T:

- Just read (parts of) the paper out loud without explaining what’s going on in your own words.
- Focus on the introduction and conclusions. (You won’t have much to discuss and won’t be able to provide a critique.)

You’ll want to aim for half the time to be focused on discussion:

- In case people are not talking, come prepared with a few discussion topics and questions.
- Try to keep the discussion focused on methods and interpreting results. Don’t spend too much time on background.
- Don’t let people go off on tangents.
